Robotic Hand R&D

Exploring automated robotic hand feasibility for factory assembly — from concept to phased validation demos

CompanySomic Ishikawa
RoleR&D Engineer
Year2024 — Present
TypeInternal R&D
R&D

Overview

An internal R&D initiative at Somic Ishikawa to explore the feasibility of an automated robotic hand for factory managers — aimed at improving efficiency by automating repeatable assembly work that currently requires human operators.

The project involved defining real-world usage scenarios, building a prototype, and running phased validation cycles to assess viability for production deployment.

R&D Capabilities

Scenario-driven validation from standing-work cycles through moving-work feasibility and a phased roadmap to production.

Standing-work validation

Defined and tested fixed-position assembly scenarios — validated repeatable left/right motion cycles demonstrating baseline motion reliability.

Moving-work feasibility

Explored dynamic assembly tasks to stress-test grip and motion under changing workpiece positions — surfacing finger-grip limits for the next engineering phase.

Scenario-driven R&D

Structured research around two core usage modes — standing vs moving work — so findings map directly to factory deployment requirements.

Phased roadmap to production

Converted validation results into a phased development plan with clear next-step requirements for future engineering iterations.

Challenges Tackled

Technical and process hurdles identified during prototype validation and scenario testing.

Finger-grip limits on dynamic tasks

Moving-work scenarios exposed grip constraints that standing cycles did not — defining hardware and control requirements before production-scale investment.

Bridging research to engineering requirements

Translating exploratory R&D findings into actionable specs for a product roadmap — separating validated capabilities from open engineering questions.

Repeatable motion reliability for factory use

Factory automation demands consistent cycle performance; validation focused on proving repeatable motion before expanding to more complex assembly paths.

Key Achievements

  • Successfully validated first-phase feasibility through repeatable standing-work motion demos
  • Defined a clear product roadmap based on identified technical limitations
  • Bridged gap between research concept and engineering requirements for future development
